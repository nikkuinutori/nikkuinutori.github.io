Photos from Wrocław Wrocław: bridges, market, history, culture, picturesque architecture, lively and lovely streets

In this photo collection, I want to show a bit of my father's interesting home country, Poland, and parts of it that many people don't know. Warsaw, Krakow and Gdańsk may be familiar to some, but western Poland is less well known, especially to Finns. Let's see some photos.

Last summer, I travelled with my father to three cities in Poland, one of which was a completely new destination for me, a very experienced traveller to Poland: the central city of Wrocław in western Poland. Wrocław, or formerly Breslau, is interesting in that it looks more like a German city than a Polish one, and no wonder. The city was part of Germany until 1945 - although there were other historical and administrative events before that. So the architecture, for example, and the general attitude to life is more 'German' than in cities like Warsaw (by which I mean that people are not as rude and oppressive as in Eastern Europe). In addition, customs and habits are different.

I have selected what I consider to be the most interesting sites and attractions in this collection, which I will also briefly present. However, the intention is not to create any kind of travel advertisement or a Tripadvisor-like "Top 10 things to see in Poland" list, but to present things freely in my own words and with my own eyes.

The Old Town

First, of course, the Old Town of Wrocław, located in the heart of the city, surrounded by the Oder River. The style of buildings is typical of Central European cities, and pretty much every major Polish city has a similar old town. Unlike Warsaw, Wrocław's old town = the centre, i.e. it is not a city centre but a separate, delimited small area in the middle of a modern city. During the Second World War, Wrocław, like many other cities, was virtually razed to the ground, although the old town in Wrocław has been better preserved than usual. So these are not the original buildings, but replicas based on pictures and drawings, which are nevertheless very impressive. Visitors to Stockholm may also notice similarities in architecture, as the style in Poland has been similar to that in Sweden because of a shared history.

The traditional layout of the old town also includes the old market area with its many restaurants and stalls. On the left you can see the former Wrocław Town Hall, which began to be built as early as the beginning of the 13th century.

Of course, the Old Town also has some beautiful churches, some of which are very old. In general, Poland probably has the largest number of old churches and ecclesiastical buildings in Europe.

Wrocław's old town is also crossed by its distinctive long shopping street, which also features newer buildings (such as the white house in this picture). In general, however, they blend in well with the old architecture and do not stand out in a disturbing way from the rest of the cityscape.

Museum Island i.e. Ostrów Tumski

Most Piaskowy, or Sand Bridge, leads to the oldest part of Wrocław, the so-called Museum Island, or in Polish also known as Ostrów Tumski, where the city was once founded. On the island you can see numerous historically significant and well-preserved buildings, such as churches, a magnificent palace (called in Polish as Pałac Arcybiskupi, as I recall) and many buildings converted into museums.

The Tumski Bridge, or Most Tumski, leading off Museum Island.

Inside of St. John the Baptist Cathedral in Wrocław

Perhaps the most decorated organ I've ever seen - I think it was in the Cathedral of St John the Baptist (i.e. Katedra św. Jana Chrzciciela in Polish).

The Wrocław Zoo and its surroundings

Wrocław Zoo's speciality is the largest "Africaarium" in Europe, which contains an impressive amount of African animals, including some I had never seen before.

In the neighbourhood of the Wrocław Zoo is the Japanese Garden, opened in 1913 by the famous German scholar and researcher of Japan, Japanese language and culture Fritz von Hochberg (1868-1921). The garden features many Japanese plants, typical Japanese wooden bridges and traditional "minka" houses (you know, those stereotypical Japanese houses).

Trams of Wrocław

Trams can be found in almost every major Polish city and they are often the best and most reliable mode of transport. By the zoo, however, the tracks were in pretty bad shape and you could feel the tracks twisted and uneven as you rode along.

The Wrocław People's Hall i.e. Jahrhunderthalle, Hala Stulecia or Hala Ludowa

This next one is really interesting: it's the Wrocław People's Hall, or Hala Stulecia or Hala Ludowa (originally Jahrhunderthalle in German).

The building is a reinforced concrete hall built in 1911-1913 to commemorate the German wars of liberation against Napoleon, and in particular the victorious battle near Breslau (Wrocław). When completed, it was the largest reinforced concrete structure in the world.

The hall's German/Polish name, Jahrhunderthalle or Hala Stulecia respectively, comes from the grand exhibition held on the day of its opening.

The building was later renamed the People's Hall after it was transferred to Poland after the Second World War in 1948. Since 2006, the site has also been a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The interior has been renovated into a museum with temporary exhibitions.

A few interesting highlights

Around the city, it is possible to find small dwarf sculptures on plots of land, usually linked in some way to the neighbourhood or street name. For example, if it were Mechanic Street, you might see a dwarf dressed as a mechanic. Or if an area is known for its restaurants, you might see a dwarf wearing a chef's hat and holding a whisk. Finding dwarves is fun for children and adults alike, and many are well hidden.

It's time to say goodbye

Our journey ended at Wrocław's castle-like main railway station, from where we continued with my father to Warsaw. Opened in 1857, it is the oldest station still in use in Poland, with architecture that resembles a castle on the outside and a market hall on the inside.
